Is there equitable access to to sufficient and healthy food across the United States? How does food insecurity compare across the nation?

The goal of our visualization is to highlight the correlations between national food insecurity and various factors, including geography, race and ethnicity, age, and access to a vehicle.

To answer our questions, we used a dataset from the U.S. Department of Agriculture within their Food Access Research Atlas. We used their most recent dataset from 2015, and wrangled and cleaned the data to remove erroneous data and exclusively focus on the columns we wanted to use for data analysis.

Although we designed our visualizations to be accessible and clear for any audience, our specific target audience are people living in the United States. It is our responsibility to ensure that residents are informed of the food insecurity in their local communities and nationwide, how these problems are exacerbated by race and class, and what individuals can do to contribute to the reduction of this problem.
Food insecurity is when an individual is not sure where their food will come from because they have little or no access to nutritious food. There are many adverse physical and mental effects of food insecurity, including hunger, malnutrition, birth defects, aggression, anxiety, and chronic illness.

For this project, we specifically measured food insecurity as it relates to food deserts. An urban food desert is a county where residents have to drive more than 1 mile to the nearest grocery store, while a rural food desert is a county where residents have to drive more than 10 miles to the nearest grocery store. Although both urban and rural communities suffer from scarce access to healthy food, urban areas usually get more assistance. Based on the limitations of our dataset and the fact that there are higher rates of food insecurity in rural food deserts, we decided to exclusively focus on rural food deserts for our analysis.

Among the 3,141 counties in the United States, 2,074 of them have at least one food desert. This means that more than 65% of the counties in the nation have limited access to affordable and healthy food.
